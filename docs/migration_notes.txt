</li></ul><h2 id="section-2012+contest+rules-Introduction">Introduction</h2>
<p>This page describes the rules for the 2012 Climate CoLab contests. Please read these rules in full. You will be required to accept them when you enter a contest.
</p>
<p>The rules may be supplemented or revised by posting supplements or revisions to this page and by email notification to members of teams entered in contests.
</p>
<p>In Climate CoLab contests, people from all over the world are invited to work together in developing proposals for what humanity should do about global climate change.
</p>
<h2 id="section-2012+contest+rules-WhatAreTheGoalsOfTheContest">What are the goals of the contest?</h2>
<p>The primary goals of the Climate CoLab contests are to:
</p>
<ul><li>Harness the collective intelligence of large numbers of people around the world to create proposals for what humanity should do about global climate change. By helping scientists, policy makers, business people, and concerned citizens to think constructively together about this problem, we believe it may be possible to create better solutions than would have been developed otherwise.
</li><li>Help to educate the general public about the real issues involved in global climate change. By giving large numbers of people an opportunity to work together themselves on proposals to address climate change, based on the best current scientific knowledge, we believe they will come to understand the issues better and be better able to participate in political decision-making in their countries and communities.
</li><li>Provide a large-scale test of new collective intelligence approaches. Examples like Wikipedia and Linux show that it’s now possible for large groups of people to work together on very complex problems in ways that would have been impossible even a decade ago. We believe the new approach to collaborative problem solving being used here may be useful for other large-scale problems in the future.
</li></ul><p>The goals of this contest are not to advocate any particular position or point of view about global climate change. Instead, we hope to provide a neutral forum where the best ideas and information can be shared.
</p>
<h2 id="section-2012+contest+rules-WhoCanParticipate">Who can participate?</h2>
<p>The contest is open to anyone in the world, regardless of age, nationality, or political viewpoint.
</p>
<p>This contest, however, is void where prohibited by law. It is your responsibility to check with your local laws to make sure that this contest does not violate any applicable law or ordinance, and to make sure that you are eligible to participate.
</p>
<p>If you are under 18 years old, you will need your parent or legal guardian to register on the Climate CoLab website and submit your entry for you.
</p>
<p>You may submit projects that you have previously published or exhibited so long as they conform to the entry guidelines and the rules of this contest.
</p>
<h2 id="section-2012+contest+rules-WhatDoesAnEntryInTheContestContain">What does an entry in the contest contain?</h2>
<p>Contest entries consist of proposals that are created and submitted online in the Climate CoLab <a class="external" href="http://www.climatecolab.org.">http://www.climatecolab.org.</a>
</p>
<p>The focus of each contest is described in the materials accompanying it, and a complete entry includes all the elements of the proposal template for a contest. 
</p>
<h2 id="section-2012+contest+rules-HowCanIEnterTheContest">How can I enter the contest?</h2>
<p>To enter, go to the main page of the contest and click on the “Create proposal” button. 
</p>
<p>After filling out the fields of the proposal template, you must save the proposal. When you save the proposal for the first time, a pop up will ask if you accept the Contest Rules and <a class="wikipage" href="http://:8888/web/guest/resources/-/wiki/Main/Terms of use">Terms of use</a>. To save the proposal and enter the contest, click on the “Accept” button. 
 
By saving and submitting a Proposal, you are agreeing to these Rules and the Terms of Use.
</p>
<p>If you created your Proposal with other individuals, all of the co-authors must sign up with the Climate CoLab website. All co-authors must be listed as members of your team, and all co-authors must understand and agree to the <a class="wikipage" href="/web/guest/resources/-/wiki/Main/Terms of use">Terms of use</a> and these Rules.
</p>
<p>The individual who initiates a proposal will be listed as the owner of that proposal and will receive all official communications about the 2012 Contest. Team members can decide among themselves who will receive any prizes that may be designated for their team should they win the contest. In cases where team members cannot agree among themselves, any prizes will be allocated to the Owner of the proposal.
</p>
<p>As set forth in the <a class="wikipage" href="/web/guest/resources/-/wiki/Main/Terms of use">Terms of use</a>, all Proposals will be made available to third parties under the <a class="external" href="http://creativecommons.org/licenses/by-nc-sa/3.0">Creative Commons Attribution-Share Alike 3.0 United States License</a>. 
</p>
<p>If you do not list any Co-Authors of the work, you are representing that you are the sole author. If you do list Co-Authors of the work, you represent that you are not violating any Co-Author’s rights by entering the work, and that any Co-Authors have given you permission to submit the work.
</p>
<h2 id="section-2012+contest+rules-WhatIsTheContestSSchedule">What is the contest’s schedule?</h2>
<p>The contest will have several stages.
</p>
<ul><li><i>Proposal creation</i> (June-October) In this phase, teams are invited to create proposals on how the world economy as a whole, as well as the economies of individual countries or trans-national groups of countries, should evolve during the 21st century in light of environmental risks. 
</li><li><i>Judging</i> (November) Expert judges will review the completed proposals and select some as finalists. For more on the judging criteria, see “How will proposals be judged,” below.
</li><li><i>Improvement of finalists</i> (Early December) Teams selected to participate in the finals will be invited to improve their proposals by addressing a set of additional issues to be specified in October.
</li><li><i>Voting</i> (Late December-January) The judges will review the finalists and decide on which proposals are eligible for voting. All registered users of the CoLab will then be invited to vote for the proposal they prefer. The judges will also select the proposals they believe are best. 
</li></ul><h2 id="section-2012+contest+rules-HowDoTeamsWork">How do teams work?</h2>
<p>Individuals may create proposals by themselves, but participants are encouraged to form teams. For instance, a team might include different people with expertise in quantitative modeling, political analysis, writing, and artistic creation.
</p>
<p>Anyone who wants to join the team creating a specific proposal can request to join that team. Then the current team members decide whether they want that person to join.
</p>
<p>If they wish, a team can restrict the right to edit its proposal to team members only. Alternatively, a team may also let anyone who is interested edit its proposals. If anyone can edit, teams can get input from lots of people without the overhead of requiring everyone to join the team. Team members can easily undo any changes they don't like.
</p>
<h2 id="section-2012+contest+rules-HowCanPeopleShowTheirSupportForAProposalBeforeTheFinalRound">How can people show their support for a proposal before the final round?</h2>
<p>At any time during the preliminary round, a member of the CoLab can become a supporter of as many proposals as they want. This makes it easy for judges and other viewers to see which proposals are most popular at each stage of the contest.
</p>
<h2 id="section-2012+contest+rules-HowCanUsersContributeNewSimulationModels">How can users contribute new simulation models?</h2>
<p>All global proposals in the 2010 and 2011 contests used simulation models included in the Climate CoLab; users are also encouraged to submit extensions to or alternatives for these models or new models that might be useful in other contests. To submit a new model, email it to rjl@mit.edu.
</p>
<p>Models submitted by users will be reviewed by the Climate CoLab staff and expert advisors and, if appropriate, added to the site where all users can use them.
</p>
<p>While it is technically possible to add many different kinds of models, the CoLab is designed to make it especially easy to add new models that are represented in spreadsheets.
</p>
<p>Users are especially encouraged to submit new spreadsheet models to calculate the inputs to the current model using more basic inputs such as emission reductions by country and/or emission reductions resulting from changes in technology mix (such as coal, solar, wind, and nuclear). For more on this, see <a class="wikipage" href="/web/guest/resources/-/wiki/Main/Models help">Models help</a>.
</p>
<h2 id="section-2012+contest+rules-HowDoesTheClimateCoLabCommunityDealWithDifferentPointsOfView">How does the Climate CoLab community deal with different points of view?</h2>
<p>The Climate CoLab is an open forum where all points of view are welcome. At the same time, the community expects members to respect facts, evidence, and rational argument.
</p>
<p>The community also expects its members to engage with each other respectfully and courteously. Failure to do this may, at the discretion of the contest organizers, result in individuals or teams being disqualified.
</p>
<h2 id="section-2012+contest+rules-HowWillProposalsBeJudged">How will proposals be judged?</h2>
<p>Judges will be asked to evaluate proposals on the following criteria:
</p>
<ul><li><i>Feasibility of the actions proposed in the proposal</i>. Judges with different kinds of expertise will evaluate the technical, economic, social, and political feasibility of the proposals. 
</li><li><i>Novelty of the proposal's ideas</i>. Innovative thinking and originality in a proposal will be valued more than encyclopedic knowledge. In addition, instead of selecting a roster of finalists that are very similar, judges will try to select a group of proposals that represent a diverse range of approaches.
</li><li><i>Likely impact on climate change</i> (for example, for mitigation actions, the amount of greenhouse gas emission reductions or for adaptation actions, the extent to which the actions counteract the effects of climate change) and <i>desirability of other impacts</i> (e.g. economic, social, lifestyle)
</li></ul>Judging criteria will be elaborated in more detail in the future. 
<ul><li><i>Presentation quality</i>. Proposals that are well-presented will be favored over those that aren't. Presentation quality includes how well written a proposal is, how well it uses graphics or other visual elements, and how compelling are its artistic representations of possible future worlds (if any).
</li></ul><p>There are no explicit weightings for these criteria, but judges will be asked to use all of them to select proposals that are most likely to lead to useful outcomes of the contest overall. As a tie- breaker, judges may also use the popularity of a proposal, as indicated by the number of people who support it. For example, in cases where a number of proposals are similar, judges will try to pick one or two proposals to represent the whole group. In selecting these representative proposals, judges will take into account the quality of the proposal presentations and the number of people who support the proposals. To increase the diversity of ideas considered, judges may accept slightly lower levels of feasibility for proposals that include highly novel and interesting ideas.
</p>
<p>In selecting proposals to move on to the voting round, judges will also be explicitly asked not to choose proposals based on their own personal preferences. In other words, judges are asked to use their expertise to judge the feasibility, novelty, and presentation quality of proposals, but not based on their perspective on what is desirable. For example, a judge should not reject a proposal that is technically, economically, and politically feasible, just because the judge feels that the proposal would lead to socially undesirable consequences.
</p>
<p>In the final round, the judges will be asked to select the proposals they believe are most desirable. Thus, judgments of desirability are made only in the final stage of the contest, by the Climate CoLab community through popular vote and by the judges through their selection of the Judges’ Choice winners.
</p>
<h2 id="section-2012+contest+rules-WhatAreThePrizes">What are the prizes?</h2>
<p>At the conclusion of contests, the winning teams and their plans will be featured on the home page of the Climate CoLab and in a press release from MIT. 
</p>
<p>Contest-specific prizes will also be awarded, as noted on the materials accompanying each contest. 
</p>
<h2 id="section-2012+contest+rules-HowWillTheResultsOfTheContestInfluencePolicy">How will the results of the contest influence policy?</h2>
<p>In connection with prior contests, the winning teams briefed relevant policy-makers about their proposals at Capitol Hill and the United Nations. This year, the contest Advisors and Fellows are working to arrange for briefings with relevant stakeholders. Information will be included in the materials accompanying each contest.</p>

				<h2 id="section-Expert+Council-WhatIsTheRoleOfTheExpertCouncil"> What is the role of the Expert Council? </h2>
<p>The Expert Council is a group of highly respected experts in various scientific and other fields related to climate change.  
</p>
<p>Their primary role is to help be sure that the best current scientific and other expert knowledge available in the world is reflected in the CoLab (see <a class="wikipage" href="http://climatecolab.org/web/guest/resources/-/wiki/Main/What do Expert Council members do">What do Expert Council members do?</a>)
</p>
<h2 id="section-Expert+Council-HowIsTheExpertCouncilSelected"> How is the Expert Council selected? </h2>
<p>All Expert Council members have published research or made other professional contributions that are respected by others in their field.  
</p>
<p>They are selected by members of the <a class="wikipage" href="http://climatecolab.org/web/guest/resources/-/wiki/Main/Expert Advisory Board">Expert Advisory Board</a> and other members of the Expert Council working with the CoLab project staff. 
</p>
<p>Some members of the Expert Council are very senior and respected members of their fields, others are more junior, but they are all viewed as leaders in their respective age groups.
</p>

				<h2 id="section-Climate+CoLab+Advisors-WhoAreClimateCoLabAdvisors">Who are Climate CoLab Advisors?</h2>
<p>Climate CoLab Advisors are experts, business executives, policy makers, non-profit leaders, and others with:
</p>
<ul><li>Deep knowledge about a key aspect of climate change, and
</li><li>An interest in helping to guide activity on <a class="external" href="http://climatecolab.org/web/guest/about">MIT's Climate CoLab</a>
</li></ul><p>In many cases, Advisors are representatives of or very knowledgeable about key stakeholders who would need to be involved in implementing proposals that emerge from the Climate CoLab process.  
</p>
<p>
</p><h2 id="section-Climate+CoLab+Advisors-WhatDoAdvisorsDo">What do Advisors do? </h2>
<p>Advisors will focus on specific contests within the Climate CoLab, and multiple advisors may assist with a single contest. Advisors will work with at least one <a class="wikipage" href="http://climatecolab.org/web/guest/resources/-/wiki/Main/Climate Colab Fellows">Climate CoLab Fellow</a>.
</p>
<p>Each contest addresses a focused topic within the broad overall realm of what to do about climate change, for example, building efficiency or adaptation finance. 
</p>
<p>Climate CoLab Advisors will contribute in four ways:
</p>
<ul><li>before a contest, help to define a question to be addressed by contest proposals and resources that would be useful to contest participants
</li><li>during a contest, provide feedback on proposals submitted by members of the Climate CoLab community and share key recent developments in the field so the community can be kept up to date,
</li><li>near the end of the contest, serve as a judge or identify peers who can serve as judges,
</li><li>throughout the process, identify other key stakeholders and help to make them aware of the contest and its results. 
</li></ul><p>
</p><h2 id="section-Climate+CoLab+Advisors-WhatIsTheTimeCommitmentForAdvisors">What is the time commitment for Advisors?</h2>
<p>The minimum time commitment expected for Advisors is about 4-8 hours per year. This time will include one-to-two hours of phone calls, typically held on a quarterly basis, with the Fellow associated with the contest area. 
</p>
<p>If Advisors choose to serve as judges, this will involve an additional 3-4 hours at the end of the contest. 
</p>
<p>Advisors are encouraged to spend as much additional time as they wish monitoring and participating in Climate CoLab activities, but this is not required to become an Advisor. 
</p>
<p>
</p><h2 id="section-Climate+CoLab+Advisors-WhatAreTheBenefitsForAdvisors">What are the benefits for Advisors?</h2>
<p>Members of the Climate CoLab community come from many different educational and other backgrounds, but all have a deep interest in what to do about climate change. As a result, the Climate CoLab is a place where real exploration and novel thinking occur. 
</p>
<p>By participating, CoLab Advisors can gauge the pulse of public opinion on issues in their areas of expertise and may even be able use the CoLab's online community to undertake work on pressing topics in their field. 
</p>
<p>In the past, the community has proven itself to be a source of novel and creative ideas for addressing climate change. By offering their guidance, Advisors will enjoy a unique opportunity to help steer a large, diverse online community of climate thinkers on issues of importance and relevance. 
</p>

				<h2 id="section-Climate+CoLab+Fellows-WhoAreClimateCoLabFellows">Who are Climate CoLab Fellows?</h2>
<p>Climate CoLab Fellows help to encourage and organize activity within a particular topic area in MIT's Climate CoLab. They are typically:
</p>
<ul><li>Postdoctoral fellows, graduate students, or others with a background in environmental science, management, or other fields related to climate change, or 
</li><li>Concerned citizens with knowledge of and interest in climate change-related issues. 
</li></ul><p>
</p><h2 id="section-Climate+CoLab+Fellows-WhatDoClimateCoLabFellowsDo">What do Climate CoLab Fellows do?</h2>
<p>Climate CoLab Fellows are assigned to a contest and matched with at least one <a class="wikipage" href="http://climatecolab.org/web/guest/resources/-/wiki/Main/Climate CoLab Advisors">Climate CoLab Advisor</a>. The Fellow will coordinate contest activity, while the Advisor will provide overall guidance. 
</p>
<p>Each contest addresses a focused topic within the broad overall realm of climate change, for example, building efficiency or adaptation finance. 
</p>
<p>A CoLab Fellow's duties are:
</p>
<ul><li>Before a contest starts, work with the Advisor to define the focus and prepare the Resources page. 
</li><li>Once a contest starts, encourage activity by reaching out to friends, groups of students and concerned citizens, NGOs, and other individuals and organizations that may have relevant interest or expertise. 
</li><li>While contests are running, moderate proposals and comments, ensuring that contributions meet the CoLab's <a class="wikipage" href="http://climatecolab.org/web/guest/resources/-/wiki/Main/Community philosophy and policies">community philosophy and policies</a>, as well as helping to resolve any disputes or disagreements that arise between community members.
</li><li>On a quarterly basis, hold a roughly one-hour call with the contest Advisor to brief them on community activity and get suggestions about how to guide the community's future work. During these review calls, it is also useful, if time allows to get feedback from the Advisor on specific proposals that seem the most promising at that stage. After the call, the Fellow will then be responsible for notifying community members of the Advisor's feedback and suggestions. 
</li><li>Near the end of the contest, undertake preliminary screening of the proposals and coordinate judging by the Advisor and/or other experts. 
</li><li>After the contest, work with the Advisor to communicate the results to organizations and individuals who are in a position to implement the best idea.  
</li><li>Throughout the process, help in identifying relevant stakeholders, informing them about the community's activities, and involving them as appropriate. 
</li></ul><p>
</p><h2 id="section-Climate+CoLab+Fellows-WhatIsTheTimeCommitmentForFellows">What is the time commitment for Fellows?</h2>
<p>Fellows are expected to spend 1-2 hours per week on average over the course of the year. The workload may be lighter while contests are ongoing and heavier around key milestones, such as launch, quarterly review, judging, and post-contest outreach to stakeholders. 
</p>
<p>
</p><h2 id="section-Climate+CoLab+Fellows-WhatAreTheBenefitsForFellows">What are the benefits for Fellows?</h2>
<p>By acting as liaison between Advisors and the community, Fellows will have an opportunity to work directly with one or more respected experts who have deep expertise in fields related to climate change. 
</p>
<p>MIT Climate CoLab Fellows will also be able to explore climate change issues of interest to them in depth, form connections with citizens, experts, and organizations engaged around those issues, and contribute to a highly innovative approach for addressing global climate change.
</p>
<p>And as more contests are launched on the Climate CoLab, Fellows will have an opportunity to interact with their fellow Fellows, a community of like-minded peers who are working together, each in their respective area of expertise, to develop creative new ways to tackle climate change. 
</p>
